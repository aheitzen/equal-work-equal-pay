History

1950's

1900 - 1950

1900’s: War brought new opportunities for women to enter the workforce. The beginning of WW1, lead to the State’s Employment Service advocating for women to fill traditionally male occupations. When women entered into these jobs, men were free to enlist to support the war effort.

1950’s: This era lead to a regression of women in the workforce. An influx of veterans needed job placement and security which caused women to be forced back into the home.

1960's

1963 - 1964

1963: John F. Kennedy signed the Equal Pay Law into effect, which aimed to abolish wage disparity based on sex. Apart of his New Frontier Program, the law was the first step in equal pay for women.

1964: The Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibited discrimination on the basis of race, origin, color, religion or sex. This bill prohibited school segregation, unlawful voter restrictions, and discrimination for employment and public accommodations.

1970's

1975 - 1978

1978: The Pregnancy Discrimination Act was an amendment to the Civil rights act of 1964. It clarified that employers could must prohibit sex discrimination on the basis of pregnancy. The act covers discrimination on the basis of pregnancy, childbirth or related mediational conditions.

1990's

1990 - 1998

1991: The Family and Medical Leave Act was passed and allowed parents, regardless of gender, to take time off. This labor law requires covered employers to provide employees with job protected and unpaid leave for familial and medical reasons.

1998: The Women's Legal Defense Fund becomes the National Partnership for Women & families, reflecting a new priority on consumer education, quality health care, and workplace issues affecting women.

2000's

2000 - 2010

2005: The Supreme Court rules that Title IX prohibits punishing someone for complaining about sex-based workplace discrimination.

2009: President Obama chose the Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act as his first piece of legislation. This act restored some protections against discrimination that had been stripped in a 2007 Supreme Court case as well as incentivizing employers to make their payrolls more fair.

LILLY LEDBETTER

For 10 years, Lilly Ledbetter fought to close the gap between women’s and men’s wages, sparring with the Supreme Court, lobbying Capitol Hill in a historic discrimination case. Ledbetter won a jury verdict of more than $3 million after having filed a gender pay discrimination suit, but the U.S. Supreme Court later overturned the lower court’s ruling. Despite her defeat, Ledbetter continued her fight until the Supreme Court decision was nullified when President Obama, on January 29, 2009, signed into law the first new law of his administration: the Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act.

LETITIA JAMES

Letitia James is an American lawyer, activist, and politician in the Democratic Party She is the current and fourth New York City Public Advocate and first African American woman to hold citywide office.The New York City Council voted to pass Public Advocate Letitia James’ groundbreaking legislation to ban employers from asking job applicants for previous salary information, a practice that perpetuates a cycle of wage discrimination. The bill, which will impact approximately 3.8 million workers, will prohibit public and private employers from requesting salary history information.

CLAUDIA GOLDIN

Claudia Goldin is an economist at Harvard University. She's a former president of the American Economic Association and a leading researcher on the gender wage gap. She is the author of the highly influential book, Understanding the Gender Gap, which challenged conventional wisdom about the root causes of unequal pay between the sexes.

CHILDCARE

The center for American progress found that women lose over $270,000 and men loose over $230,000 in lifetime wages and benefits due to leaving the workforce early for caregiving responsibilities. We can help combat this economic loss by ensuring that families, men and women, have access to safe and affordable childcare.

One of the key findings that causes the wage gap is child rearing. Women are more likely than men to take on caregiving responsibilities and in that they lose time they could be spending actively in the workforce. When women, especially women who make minimum wage, have access to affordable childcare they are able to stay in the workforce longer which can narrow the wage gap.

minimum wage

Two-thirds of minimum wage workers are women. Raising the minimum wage would not only positively impact women who work in low wage and tipped occupations, but it would vastly improve fair pay towards women of color.

Alongside raising the minimum wage, more emphasises should be placed on pay negotiation and imposter syndrome, and how these ideas negatively affect women's potential earnings. According to research from the National Women’s law center, 57% of men negotiate their salaries compared to only 7% of women. Pay negotiation is tricky for women because some behaviors that are seen as positives for men, such as self-promotion, frequently backfire against women.
